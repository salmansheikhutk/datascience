Summary

What is K‑Means? It’s a way to automatically group data points that look similar, without using any labels. Here we asked it to form 2 groups (k=2), hoping one group lines up with benign tumors and the other with malignant tumors.

How we evaluated it: We didn’t use the diagnosis labels to train K‑Means. After clustering, we checked how well the two groups match the real labels (this is a post‑hoc check). We also used a standard unsupervised score called the silhouette score to see how well the groups are separated.

Results: The silhouette score is 0.3434 (higher means cleaner separation). When we align each cluster to the majority label, we get 90.51% accuracy, and the Adjusted Rand Index (which measures agreement between clusters and labels) is 0.6536. The confusion matrix shows there were 18 benign cases grouped with malignant and 36 malignant cases grouped with benign.

Takeaway: Even without labels, K‑Means finds structure that largely corresponds to the medical diagnosis. However, it’s not as accurate as a supervised model trained with labels (like Logistic Regression), so it’s best used for exploration, not final diagnosis.

Details

Steps:

  • Imputed missing values and standardized all features
  • Ran K‑Means with k=2
  • Computed silhouette score (unsupervised quality)
  • Mapped each cluster to the majority class, then computed accuracy and ARI
